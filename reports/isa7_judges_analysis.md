## Overview

On the Bible Wheel, Isaiah chapter 7 — the Immanuel prophecy, Ahaz and the Syro-Ephraimite war — corresponds to Judges, book #7, the book of Israel's deliverance cycles. The connection is unusually deep: Isaiah 7 draws its two most famous elements — the Immanuel name and the birth-annunciation — directly from the language of the Judges deliverer-narratives (Gideon and Samson). The Immanuel prophecy is, at its linguistic roots, a Judges deliverance-oracle reborn in the house of David.

## Connection 1 — Isa 7:14 // Judges 13:3-7 | Grade AAA | Score 24

**The birth-annunciation: "Behold, you shall conceive and bear a son"**

Isaiah 7:14 is cast in the exact annunciation formula used for the birth of Samson — a form that appears in only a handful of places in the Hebrew Bible (Gen 16:11; Judg 13:3,5,7; Isa 7:14).

| Isa 7:14 | Judg 13:5,7 |
|---|---|
| hinneh ha-almah harah ve-yoledet ben | hinnakh harah ve-yoladt ben |
| "Behold, the young woman conceiving and bearing a son" | "Behold, you are conceiving and shall bear a son" |
| "and shall call his name (qarat shemo) Immanuel" | "the boy shall be a Nazirite... he shall begin to save Israel" |

The shared roots are harah (conceive/pregnant, H2030 — the rarer adjectival form), yalad (bear, H3205), and ben (son), in the same word-order formula, each followed by the child's divinely-given name/destiny. Crucially, both children are deliverer-figures: Samson "shall begin to save (yasha) Israel from the Philistines"; Immanuel is the sign that the threat against Judah "shall not stand." Isaiah deliberately frames the Immanuel-child in the mold of the Judges deliverer-annunciation.

## Connection 2 — Isa 7:10-14 // Judges 6:11-17 | Grade AAA | Score 23

**The sign (oth) and "YHWH is with us" — the source of the Immanuel name**

The most theologically striking connection. The very phrase behind Immanu-el ("God-with-us") is spoken, verbatim, by Gideon.

| Isa 7:11-14 | Judg 6:12-17 |
|---|---|
| "Ask a sign (oth, H226) of YHWH" / "the Lord will give you a sign (oth)" | "show me a sign (oth, H226) that it is you who speak with me" |
| name him Immanu-el = "God with us" (immanu) | "YHWH is with you (YHWH immkha)" / "if YHWH is with us (immanu), why...?" |
| Ahaz refuses to ask, "I will not test YHWH" | Gideon hesitates, asks for proof |
| to the house of David in a national crisis | to Gideon at the Midianite crisis |

Gideon's doubting question — "if YHWH is with us (YHWH immanu), why has all this happened?" (Judg 6:13) — contains the exact phrase that becomes the name Immanu-el in Isaiah 7:14. Both scenes pair the offer/request of a sign (oth) with the assurance that God is with his people at a moment of threatened faith. Isaiah's Immanuel answers Gideon's question across the centuries: God is with us.

**Scores:** Terms 6 | Themes 9 | Thesis 8 | Total 23
**Anchor:** oth "sign" (H226) shared; the immanu / immkha "with us/you" phrase — literally the Immanuel name, spoken by Gideon.

**Swarming invaders devour the land like insects**

| YHWH whistles for the fly (zevuv) of Egypt and the bee (devorah) of Assyria — they "all come and settle" | Midian, Amalek, the people of the East come up "like locusts (arbeh) for multitude" |

Both depict foreign invaders swarming like insects over the land to devastate it, with the same livestock pair shor + seh (ox + sheep, both uncommon words) and the land reduced to ruin. Isaiah's fly-and-bee simile for Egypt and Assyria matches Judges' locust simile for Midian.

## Connection 4 — Isa 7:1-9 // Judges 6:1-6 | Grade AA | Score 17

**The enemy coalition, the terror, and the question of deliverance**

| Isa 7:1-9 | Judg 6:1-6 |
|---|---|
| Rezin (Aram) + Pekah (Ephraim) come up (alah) against Jerusalem | Midian + Amalek + the East come up (alah) against Israel |
| Ahaz's heart "shook like the trees of the forest before the wind" (terror) | Israel "brought very low," made dens in the mountains (terror) |
| "Be quiet, do not fear... it shall not stand" | the people "cried out (zaaq) to YHWH" for rescue |

Both open the same way: a foreign coalition comes up (alah) against God's people, who are gripped by terror, and the narrative turns on whether YHWH will deliver. Isaiah's "fear not, it shall not stand" is the prophetic counterpart to Judges' deliverance-cycle, where the cry of the oppressed brings a savior.

## Connection 5 — Isa 7:3-9 // Judges 7:2-7 | Grade A | Score 15

**Deliverance by faith, not by human strength**

| Isa 7:4-9 | Judg 7:2-7 |
|---|---|
| "If you are not firm in faith (im lo ta'aminu), you will not be established (lo te'amenu)" — the aman/aman wordplay | "The people are too many... lest Israel boast, 'My own hand has saved me'" |
| Ahaz must simply believe to be secure | Gideon's army cut from 22,000 to 300 so the victory is God's |

Both teach that deliverance rests not on human might but on trusting YHWH alone: Ahaz needs only to believe (aman) to be established; Gideon's force is whittled to 300 so that "faith, not the hand of man, saves." A conceptual rather than verbal parallel, but a genuine shared theology of faith-over-strength.
