## Overview

On the Bible Wheel, Isaiah chapter 24 corresponds to Jeremiah — book #24. Isaiah 24 is the great cosmic-judgment vision ("the earth is utterly emptied and laid waste"), and Jeremiah is the prophet of the land's desolation. They share five tight lexical clusters — including the single most famous verbatim allusion in the Hebrew prophets. The shared vocabulary forms a recognizable judgment-lexicon: aval (the earth mourns), alah (the curse), chanaph (defiled), tohu (chaos/de-creation), shavat (joy/wine ceases), and the unique pachad/pachat/pach triplet. Isaiah 24 is the cosmic template; Jeremiah is its historical outworking on Judah and the nations.

**The terror / pit / snare triplet — the benchmark unique allusion**

פַּחַד וָפַחַת וָפָח עָלֶיךָ — pachad va-pachat va-pach — "Terror and pit and snare are upon you."

This alliterative three-word formula appears in ONLY these two verses in the entire Hebrew Bible, in near-identical word order, followed by the same cascading escape-sequence.

| Isa 24:17-18 | Jer 48:43-44 |
|---|---|
| pachad (H6343) + pachat (H6354) + pach (H6341) | pachad + pachat + pach — identical |
| "he who flees (nus) from the terror shall fall (naphal) into the pit" | "he who flees from the terror shall fall into the pit" |
| "he who climbs out (alah) of the pit shall be caught (lakad) in the snare" | "he who climbs out of the pit shall be caught in the snare" |

Shared content roots: H6343 pachad, H6354 pachat, H6341 pach, H5127 nus (flee), H5307 naphal (fall), H5927 alah (climb), H3920 lakad (caught) — seven shared roots, four of them the verbs of the escape-cascade. The only difference is the addressee: Isaiah's cosmic "inhabitant of the earth," Jeremiah's "inhabitant of Moab." Jeremiah applies Isaiah's universal apocalyptic oracle to a specific nation's day of reckoning.
