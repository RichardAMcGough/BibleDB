## Overview

Isaiah 42 is Luke's primary Servant Christology source. What makes this distinctive is that Matthew quotes Isaiah 42:1-4 explicitly (Matt 12:17-21), but Luke uses it more subtly and more architecturally — positioning Isaiah 42 allusions at every structural hinge of the Gospel: Birth, Presentation, Baptism, Manifesto, Credentials, and Transfiguration. The most striking discovery is Luke's uniquely precise wording at the Transfiguration (9:35), where he substitutes "the Chosen One" for Matthew and Mark's "the Beloved" — matching Isaiah 42:1 LXX more exactly than either of the other Gospels.

## Connection 1 — Isa 42:1 // Luke 9:35 | Grade AAA+ | Score 27

**The Transfiguration — "My Chosen One": Luke's editorial signature**

This is the most important single piece of evidence for Luke's Isaiah 42 Christology, and it is **uniquely Lucan**:

| Gospel | Transfiguration Voice | LXX Source |
|---|---|---|
| Matt 17:5 | "ὁ **ἀγαπητός**" (my beloved) | Ps 2:7 / Isa 42:1 agapetos |
| Mark 9:7 | "ὁ **ἀγαπητός**" (my beloved) | Ps 2:7 / Isa 42:1 agapetos |
| **Luke 9:35** | **"ὁ ἐκλελεγμένος" (my Chosen One)** | **Isa 42:1 LXX: ὁ ἐκλεκτός μου** |

Luke alone substitutes *beloved* (ἀγαπητός) with **the Chosen One** (ἐκλελεγμένος) — the perfect passive participle of ἐκλέγομαι — precisely matching Isaiah 42:1 LXX's ὁ ἐκλεκτός μου (my chosen/elect one). This is a deliberate editorial decision. Luke is identifying Jesus as Isaiah's *bechiri* (בְּחִירִי, my chosen one) with greater precision than the other Gospels.

The Transfiguration is Luke's theological center — the point where Moses (Law) and Elijah (Prophets) yield to Jesus. Luke marks this moment of supreme disclosure with the LXX's distinctive word for the Servant's election. The closing command "listen to him" (ἀκούετε αὐτοῦ) echoes Isa 42:4: "the coastlands wait for his teaching/law."

**Scores:** Terms 9 | Themes 9 | Thesis 9 | Total 27  
**LXX anchor:** ἐκλελεγμένος (Luke 9:35) / ἐκλεκτός (Isa 42:1 LXX) — same root; unique editorial substitution found only in Luke  
**Themes:** Divine election of the Servant at the Gospel's theological center; the Servant superseding Moses and Elijah; the cloud/voice echoing Sinai's divine disclosure; "listen to him" as the Servant's authoritative teaching replacing the Law  
**Thesis:** Luke's choice of ἐκλελεγμένος over ἀγαπητός is his clearest editorial declaration that Jesus is Isaiah 42's Servant. The word is unintelligible without Isa 42:1 LXX as its source. This single word choice unlocks Luke's entire Servant Christology.

## Connection 2 — Isa 42:1 // Luke 3:22 | Grade AAA | Score 25

The baptism voice composites Psalm 2:7 ("You are my Son") with Isaiah 42:1, enacting all three elements of the Servant's commissioning simultaneously:

## Connection 3 — Isa 42:6 // Luke 2:32 | Grade AA+ | Score 22

**Simeon's Nunc Dimittis — "A light for revelation to the Gentiles"**

Isa 42:6 LXX: **φῶς ἐθνῶν** — "light of nations"  
Luke 2:32: **φῶς εἰς ἀποκάλυψιν ἐθνῶν** — "light for revelation to the Gentiles"

Both Isa 42:6 and 49:6 contain the "light of the nations" formula. Isa 42:6 is the primary source for Luke 2:32 because:

1. Isa 42:7 immediately follows with "to open the eyes that are blind" — the ἀποκάλυψιν (revelation/opening) of Luke 2:32 resonates with this; *revelation* implies the opening of blind eyes to what was previously unseen
2. Luke has been building an Isa 42 tapestry from 1:79 onward; the Simeon scene fits the ascending arc
3. Isa 42:6 has "I will take you by the hand and keep you" — Simeon *holds* the infant Jesus in his arms, a striking literal resonance with YHWH taking the Servant by the hand

Luke 2:32 is the theological climax of the Infancy Narrative, announcing the Gentile theme that will dominate Luke-Acts.

**Scores:** Terms 6 | Themes 8 | Thesis 8 | Total 22  
**LXX anchor:** φῶς ἐθνῶν (light of nations/Gentiles); ἀποκάλυψιν echoing "open eyes" of Isa 42:7  
**Themes:** Light for the Gentiles as Servant mission from birth; Simeon as prophetic witness to the Servant's appointment; glory to Israel alongside revelation to the nations; the infant Servant already carrying global commission  
**Thesis:** Without Isa 42:6, Simeon's declaration is a pious hope; with it, the Gentile mission is the *original* commission of the Servant now being fulfilled in the infant Jesus, guaranteeing that Luke-Acts's reach to Rome is not a deviation but the fulfillment of Isaiah's first Servant Song.

## Connection 4 — Isa 42:1-7 // Luke 4:16-21 | Grade AA | Score 19

**Nazareth Manifesto — Isaiah 42 as the sub-text beneath Isaiah 61**

Luke 4:18 explicitly quotes **Isaiah 61:1-2**. But Isaiah 42:1-7 is the structural background that explains what Luke 4 is doing:

| Isa 42 | Luke 4 |
|---|---|
| v.1: "I have put my Spirit upon him" | v.18: "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me" |
| v.7: "to open the eyes that are blind" | v.18: "recovery of sight to the blind" (τυφλοῖς ἀνάβλεψιν) |
| v.7: "to bring out prisoners from the dungeon" | v.18: "to set at liberty those who are oppressed" |
| v.7: "those who sit in darkness" | v.18: "oppressed" — captivity/darkness semantics |
| v.6: "a light for the nations" | v.25-27: mission to Zarephath (Elijah) and Naaman (Elisha) — Gentiles! |

The decisive evidence is **Luke 4:25-27**: Jesus expands the Isa 61 quotation to include the Gentiles — Elijah's widow of Zarephath; Elisha's Naaman the Syrian. This expansion is not from Isa 61. It comes from **Isa 42:6's "light for the nations."** The Nazareth pericope composites Isa 61 (the explicit text) with Isa 42's Servant program (the Gentile mission). This is what enrages the crowd: they accept the Isaiah 61 program (healing for Israel) but reject the Isaiah 42 extension (mission to the Gentiles).

**Scores:** Terms 4 | Themes 8 | Thesis 7 | Total 19  
**LXX anchor:** πνεῦμα κυρίου ἐπ᾽ ἐμέ (Spirit of Lord upon me) in both Isa 42:1 and 61:1; τυφλοῖς ἀνάβλεψιν closer to Isa 42:7's "open eyes that are blind"  
**Themes:** Spirit-anointing of the Servant; healing blindness; releasing the captives; "today this Scripture is fulfilled" as Servant-inauguration; Gentile expansion proving the Isa 42 program is operative beneath Isa 61  
**Thesis:** The Gentile expansion in Luke 4:25-27 (which nearly gets Jesus killed) is the literary climax of the Nazareth pericope. Its warrant is Isa 42:6's "light for the nations" — not Isa 61. Isa 42 is the sub-text that explains *why* Jesus extends the mission to Gentiles at this inaugural moment.

## Connection 7 — Isa 42:3 // Luke's Portrait of Jesus | Grade B+ | Score 12

**"A bruised reed he will not break" — Luke's distinctive gentleness**

Isa 42:3: "a bruised reed he will not break, and a smoldering wick he will not quench; he will faithfully bring forth justice."

This is not explicitly quoted in Luke, but Luke 4:18 uses συντετριμμένους (the broken/crushed ones) from the same root (τρίβω/crush) as Isa 42:3 LXX's τετριμμένον (bruised/crushed reed). More importantly, Luke's Gospel is the sustained literary embodiment of Isa 42:3 in the NT — his distinctive emphasis on the broken whom Jesus restores rather than condemns:

- **The sinful woman** (7:36-50) — Jesus does not break the bruised reed
- **Zacchaeus** (19:1-10) — the smoldering wick sought out and rekindled  
- **The prodigal son** (15:11-32) — the father running to meet the broken one
- **The woman with the bent back** (13:10-17) — released after 18 years
- **The thief on the cross** (23:43) — restored at the last moment
- **The Samaritan leper** (17:11-19) — welcomed by the one everyone despised

Luke's Gospel is the most sustained literary enactment of Isa 42:3's portrait of the Servant who does not crush the wounded but restores them.

**Themes:** Gentleness with the broken; restoration rather than condemnation; the outsider welcomed; Luke's consistent social reversals  
**Thesis:** Isa 42:3 is the thematic background for Luke's portrait of Jesus across the whole Gospel, not a specific quotation.

## Why Luke and Not Matthew?

Matthew explicitly quotes Isaiah 42:1-4 in full (Matt 12:17-21): "Behold my servant whom I have chosen, my beloved in whom my soul is well pleased; I will put my Spirit upon him." This makes Matthew's Isaiah 42 dependence obvious and explicit.

Luke never quotes Isaiah 42:1-4 explicitly — yet he uses it more architecturally, more pervasively, and at moments of greater structural importance. Luke is the Gospel that most fully *enacts* the Servant's program: Spirit anointing, healing the blind, releasing the oppressed, bringing light to the Gentiles, and refusing to break the bruised reed. Isaiah 42 is not a prooftext for Luke — it is the **template** by which Luke shapes his entire portrait of Jesus.
