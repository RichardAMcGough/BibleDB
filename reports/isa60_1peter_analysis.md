## Overview

Isaiah 60:1-10 and 1 Peter 2:1-10 share a strong thematic allusion centered on the **light-darkness-proclamation sequence**. Isaiah 60 is not explicitly quoted in 1 Peter 2 — Peter's explicit OT quotations in this passage are from Isa 28:16, Ps 118:22, Isa 8:14, Exod 19:6, Isa 43:20-21, and Hosea 1-2. Nevertheless, Isaiah 60 forms the eschatological canvas on which Peter's ecclesiology is painted, and the light → proclamation logic of 1 Pet 2:9b is most fully intelligible against Isa 60's backdrop.

---

## The Core Connection: Light → Proclamation

Isaiah 60's structural logic:

1. **v.1-2**: YHWH's light rises over Zion; darkness covers the nations
2. **v.3**: Nations walk by Zion's light to YHWH
3. **v.6**: Nations bring offerings; *they proclaim the praise of YHWH* (יְבַשֵּׂרוּ תְהִלֹּת יְהוָה)

1 Peter 2:9's structural logic (the climax of the entire passage):

> "But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession, **that you may proclaim the excellencies** (ἐξαγγείλητε τὰς ἀρετάς) **of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light**."

The sequence is **structurally identical**:
- Called/drawn into YHWH's light (Isa 60:1-2) → *so that you proclaim* (Isa 60:6)
- Called out of darkness into his marvelous light (1 Pet 2:9a) → *so that you proclaim* (1 Pet 2:9b)

The "so that you proclaim" clause is the theological *telos* of both passages — the community of light exists in order to declare YHWH's excellencies/praises to the nations.

## Thematic Analysis

### A. Light/Darkness → Proclamation sequence | +3

The tightest structural parallel. Both passages use the same logic: the community is constituted by the arrival of YHWH's light into darkness, and this light-identity obligates proclamation. This is not merely a shared metaphor — it is a shared **theological sequence** in which the experience of divine light produces the mission to declare.

### B. Priestly service / acceptable sacrifices | +2

- **Isa 60:7**: Nations bring flocks "accepted on my altar" (יַעֲלוּ עַל-רָצוֹן מִזְבְּחִי / LXX: *euprosdekta* in some manuscripts)
- **1 Pet 2:5**: "offer spiritual sacrifices **acceptable** (εὐπροσδέκτους) to God"

The *ratsôn*-acceptance vocabulary (favor/acceptance of offerings) maps onto Peter's "acceptable sacrifices." The community as a holy priesthood offering acceptable sacrifices echoes Isa 60:7's vision of nations bringing acceptable offerings to YHWH's altar.

### C. Holy community / people identity | +2

- **Isa 60:9**: "for the name of YHWH your God and the Holy One of Israel"
- **1 Pet 2:9**: "a chosen race, a royal priesthood, **a holy nation**, a people for his own possession"

While Peter's primary source for the election titles is Exod 19:6 + Isa 43:20-21, Isaiah 60's vision of a purified, holy, priestly Zion to which nations stream provides the eschatological frame these titles inhabit.

### D. Ingathering / "coming to" motif | +1

- **Isa 60:3-5**: Nations *come* to Zion's light
- **1 Pet 2:4**: Believers *come* (προσερχόμενοι) to the living stone

## Why Isa 60, Not Just Exod 19 and Isa 43?

Peter's explicit OT scaffolding in 2:1-10 builds on:
- A **stone catena**: Isa 28:16 + Ps 118:22 + Isa 8:14
- An **election catena**: Exod 19:6 + Isa 43:20-21 + Hosea 1-2

Isa 43:21 is Peter's explicit source for "proclaim my praises" (θαυμαστὸν φῶς and ἐξαγγείλητε τὰς ἀρετάς). So why does Isa 60 matter?

Because Isa 60 provides the **eschatological vision** that gives the proclamation imperative its cosmic scope. In Isa 43:21, God forms a people "to declare my praise" — but the audience is unspecified. In Isa 60, the audience is the **nations** streaming to Zion's light. The "so that you may proclaim" in 1 Pet 2:9 is not merely worship — it is **missionary proclamation to the world**, and that cosmic missionary scope comes from Isa 60, not Isa 43 alone.

Peter is compositing Isaiah 43 (the election/proclamation promise) and Isaiah 60 (the eschatological light/nations context) into a single theological claim about the church's identity and mission.
